When the Brumadinho tailings dam collapsed in Brazil in January 2019, killing 270 people, investigators found that satellite thermal imagery had captured elevated temperatures in the months before failure. The dam's internal structure was degrading through seepage and piping, releasing frictional heat detectable by MODIS sensors from 705 km in space.

This tutorial uses Apache Sedona + Databricks for mine-site thermal monitoring by processing 25 years of MODIS Land Surface Temperature data across Western Australia. It does a spatial join of 2.3M pixels × 847 mine polygons executing in 2.4 minutes using Sedona spatial indexing, per-pixel z-score anomaly detection against multi-year baselines, and automated alerting on sustained temperature elevations.

Mining companies operate thousands of tailings dams, waste rock dumps, and leach pads across remote terrain where thermal anomalies signal safety risks: spontaneous combustion, acid drainage, dam seepage, or heap leach failures. Traditional monitoring relies on sporadic field visits. Satellites image every mine site on Earth every 8 days—the challenge is engineering systems that surface anomalies in seconds.
